Frequently Asked Questions

How many genes in Chromosome 3 genome have been submitted to GenBank?

Chromosome 3 spans 198,295,559 base pairs. There are 2,971 genes of which 1,071 are protein coding genes.
As on 09 April 2017 NCBI

How many genes from Chromosome 3 does the RGR3 database include?

RGR3 Genome Browser uses the data from Genbank and comprises a select group of 386 protein coding genes from Homo sapiens Chromosome 3 genome.

Which genes does RGR3 include?

The small percentage of coding sequences that require stretches of DNA to be joined from another entry to form the complete coding sequence, have not been selected for inclusion. Similarly, mutations, variations, and alternative splicing have also been omitted from the RGR3 database. In the case of alternative splicing, the first splice variant has been selected. For a full list of the genes in RGR3 database please refer back to the home page summary list.

Why are the Gene Identifier and GenBank Accession codes different?

Gene Identifier: A unique numeric identifier given to each sequence record processed by NCBI (National Centre for Biotechnology Information). GenBank Accession: A unique identifier comprising of five digits preceeded by two letters, given to a sequence when it is submitted to the GenBank DNA repository, in line with the International Nucleotide Sequence Database Collaboration.

Does the RGR3 database contain codon frequencies?

Yes. If you wish to compare the codon bias between genomes then please look at the Chromosome 3 codon frequency table. To compare codon bias between an individual gene and the genome average then select the codon frequencies checkbox on the homepage.

Why compare codon frequencies within the genome?

Synonymous codon usage biases are associated with various biological factors, such as gene expression level, gene length, gene translation initiation signal, protein amino acid composition, protein structure, tRNA abundance, mutation fre-quency and patterns, and GC compositions. Quantification of codon usage bias helps understand evolution of living organisms.

What makes a 'good' sticky end restriction enzyme?

RGR3 classes an enzyme as 'good' if it cuts the gene outside of the coding region to avoid disrupting the coding DNA segment.

Can I analyse more than one sticky end restriction enzyme?

To analyse more than one sticky end restriction enzyme at a time, simply hold down the shift key whilst pressing the down and/or up arrow keys on the keyboard to make your selection.

What browser should I use?

RGR3 Genome Browser is written to the lastest standards of HTML5 and CSS3 (or CSS2.1-Help page). Please ensure that your preferred browser supports these standards and video .mp4 elements.
